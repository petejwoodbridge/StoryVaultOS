INT. ALEX'S APARTMENT — NIGHT

The apartment is small and deliberately bare. No furniture that wasn't functional.
One wall is covered floor-to-ceiling in acoustic spectrograms, pinned edge-to-edge —
a mosaic of sound rendered as image, frequencies made visible.

ALEX CHEN (32) stands at the kitchen counter in her coat, still buttoned. She holds
a letter. Reads it. Reads it again.

She sets it on the counter. Opens a drawer. Takes out a worn notebook — waterproof
cover, edges soft with use, a pen clipped to the front.

She carries the notebook to the wall of spectrograms. Stands in front of it without
looking at it. Opens the notebook to the last written page.

We can't read what's there. She can.

She stands there a long time.

                                        ALEX (V.O.)
                    They want the notebook back in seventy-two hours.
                    That's a very specific number.
                    Seventy-two hours is the window before a Mnemix
                    encoding degrades past legal admissibility.

She closes the notebook. Folds the letter in half and puts it in her coat pocket.

Then she takes out her phone. Scrolls to a name we can't see. Dials.

It rings. And rings.

                                        ALEX
                    I know you said not to call this number.
                    Something's changed.

She listens to the reply. Her face gives nothing away.

                                        ALEX (CONT'D)
                    Then we meet tonight.

She ends the call. Looks at the spectrograms on the wall.

Somewhere in the mosaic, barely visible, is one sheet different from the others —
hand-drawn, not printed. A pattern of curves and intervals that doesn't match any
of the others.

She doesn't look at it directly. She doesn't need to.

She leaves.
